Room Acoustics & Reverb — Background
Ray Tracing

When a sound source emits energy it radiates outward in all directions. This simulator fires a configurable number of rays from the source at evenly-spaced angles. Each ray travels at the speed of sound (343 m/s) and reflects specularly off each wall, losing energy proportional to that wall's absorption coefficient on every bounce.

A ray contributes to the impulse response whenever it passes within 0.5 m of the listener position. The arrival time is the total path length divided by the speed of sound; the amplitude is the product of all reflection attenuations divided by distance.

Impulse Response

The impulse response (IR) is the room's acoustic fingerprint: the output you would hear if the source emitted a perfect instantaneous click. It is built here directly from the ray arrivals — each contributing a spike at its arrival time with amplitude scaled by attenuation and distance.

The direct sound arrives first (positive polarity). Each subsequent wall reflection inverts polarity, so odd-numbered reflections appear below the zero line. Later arrivals cluster into the reverberant tail.

RT60 ≈ 0.161 × V / A   (Sabine)

V = room volume (m³), A = total absorption area. RT60 is the time for the sound to decay by 60 dB.

Schroeder Reverberator (1962)

Manfred Schroeder's landmark 1962 paper "Natural Sounding Artificial Reverberation" showed that a convincing reverb tail could be synthesised from a bank of parallel feedback comb filters followed by a cascade of allpass filters. The comb filters create the dense echo build-up; the allpass filters diffuse it without colouring the frequency response.

This implementation uses four parallel comb filters summed and fed through two series allpass sections, matching Schroeder's original topology.

Feedback Comb Filter

A delay line of length D samples with its output fed back through a gain g. Each pass through the loop attenuates the signal by g and delays it by D samples, producing a series of equally-spaced echoes with exponentially decaying amplitude.

y[n] = x[n−D] + g · y[n−D]

The loop gain g controls the decay rate. RT60 is approximately −3D / log₁₀(g) seconds.

Allpass Filter

The allpass section has unity gain at every frequency — it does not colour the sound. It introduces frequency-dependent phase shifts that smear the echoes in time, increasing perceptual density without changing the spectral envelope.

y[n] = −g·x[n] + x[n−D] + g·y[n−D]

|H(ejω)| = 1 for all ω. Two stages in series raise the echo density further.

Parameter Guide
Comb delay D

Sets the fundamental echo spacing. Four different delays (typically prime multiples) spread the resonances spectrally, avoiding metallic colouration.

Comb gain g

Controls decay rate per comb. Higher g = longer tail. Should remain below 1.0 for stability. Values around 0.8–0.9 give natural-sounding RT60s.

Allpass g

Controls diffusion. Higher values spread the echoes more widely in time. Schroeder recommended g ≈ 0.7 as a perceptual optimum.
